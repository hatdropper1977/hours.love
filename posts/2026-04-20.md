The sorting table on Graton Road vibrates at a frequency that rattles teeth. I stand on a metal platform at 5 AM. Sodium lights cut through Sebastopol fog. A plastic bin tips over the hopper. Three tons of Pinot Noir spill onto the moving white belt. Cold juice splashes my chin. It smells of damp earth and crushed berries. My job is to find the MOG. Material Other than Grapes. I pull out yellow jackets, dried leaves, and blue plastic netting.

The belt moves six inches per second. Do not look away. Blink and a cluster of shriveled raisins slips into the de-stemmer. These raisins make wine taste like prune juice. Blue nitrile gloves stick together from sugar. My fingers feel heavy. Wind pulls heat from the concrete floor and my damp flannel sleeves.

Mario stands opposite me. He has worked thirty harvests in the Russian River Valley. He uses his hands more than his eyes. He feels for the wetness of botrytis. He pulls a cluster covered in grey fuzz and throws it into a red bucket. The de-stemmer motor drowns out conversation. We wear foam earplugs. The silence inside your head makes the visual task harder. Purple roundness becomes a blur.

At noon, sun hits stainless steel tanks. The glare is blinding. A forklift driver brings a fresh bin from the lower vineyard. It contains a mummified lizard and rusted shears lost months ago. I toss the lizard by the tail into the cover crop. These grapes come from a block called The Hook. The vines are forty years old. They grow in Goldridge soil. Fine sand gets into boots and stays until December.

The fruit looks different than lab reports. Paper says 24 Brix. My tongue says acid remains sharp. We taste a grape every ten minutes to keep the palate focused. Skins are thick this year. We finish the last bin at 4 PM. My lower back feels like a hot wire. I spray the belt with a high-pressure hose. Purple water runs into the floor drain. Yeast starts in tank four tonight. Tomorrow we repeat the process with Chardonnay.

Sorting is exclusion. You remove distractions to find the wine. We move 12 tons a day. Each ton requires thousands of decisions. A green stem goes left. A clean cluster goes right. Your brain stops thinking about the bottle. It thinks about the next six inches of belt. Harvest pressure is not the weather. It is the rhythm. If you stop, the cellar stops.
