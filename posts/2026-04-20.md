The mobile bottling truck backed into the Sebastopol warehouse at 6:00 AM. Mike, the operator, drank Mountain Dew from a warm bottle. He did not talk. He hooked up nitrogen lines and started the diesel generator. The roar made the metal walls vibrate. We had twelve pallets of Syrah to move into glass.

I worked the dump table. You grab a case of empty bottles. You flip it over the stainless steel rails. The glass slides onto the conveyor. Cardboard dust fills the air. The bottles are cold. Paper shavings sit under your fingernails. If you miss a beat, the machine chirps. If you rush, the glass jams and explodes.

The air smelled like sulfur and ozone. By 10:00 AM, the warehouse hit eighty-five degrees. The Syrah was fifty-eight degrees. Condensation coated the filler tubes. Mike adjusted the labeler with a greasy wrench. He watched the spools. A three-millimeter tilt ruins the brand. We used dead leaf green glass from Mexicali. The corks came from Portugal. They smelled like a damp cellar.

Lunch was carnitas tacos from the truck on Gravenstein Highway. We sat on stacked pallets. No one talked about the fruit or oak. We talked about the glue heater. It kept clogging. My lower back throbbed. Every empty case weighs twenty-two pounds. Full cases weigh thirty-eight. You lift three hundred cases an hour. Your shoulders lock by noon.

At 2:00 PM, a bottle shattered in the corking chamber. Red wine sprayed the white curtains. It looked like an artery had opened. We shut down the line. I used a squeegee to push the Syrah toward the drain. The wine was sticky. It turned my boot soles into magnets for cardboard scraps. We scrubbed the broken glass out of the gears.

The last case went through the taper at 5:45 PM. Mike unhooked the braided hoses. He rinsed the filler bowls with caustic soda. The truck pulled away. The warehouse felt cavernous. Four pallets of finished wine stood in the center of the room. My hands were stained purple. The skin on my palms felt thin from the acid.

I did not taste the Syrah. It has bottle shock. The oxygen ruins the flavor for weeks. It is just heavy inventory now. I drank a cold pilsner from a can. My ears still rang. I drove home with the windows down to clear the smell of wet cardboard.
