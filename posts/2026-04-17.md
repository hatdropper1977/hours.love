The mud in Philo sticks to rubber boots until they weigh ten pounds. I stood in Block 4 of a hillside vineyard yesterday. Rain hit the back of my neck. My shears felt like ice. Pruning is not a romantic ritual. It is a repetitive motion that makes hands cramp by noon.

I cut back canes on ten year old Pinot Noir vines. The wood feels brittle. You choose two fruiting canes and two replacement spurs for next season. One wrong cut kills the yield for that position. I worked next to a man named Javier. He moved three times faster than me. He didn't wear gloves. His calluses looked like leather. He used Felco 2 shears. Mine felt heavy by the fourth row.

The Anderson Valley stays colder than Napa. Fog sits in the redwoods until two in the afternoon. Dampness feeds moss on the north side of the trunks. We cleared brush into the center of the rows. A flail mower chews it into mulch later. The soil here is Bearwallow-Wolfey. It is gravelly but the surface stays a slick paste after a storm.

Lunch was a turkey sandwich in the cab of a white Ford truck. The heater blew dust into my eyes. We drank lukewarm coffee from a metal thermos. No one talked about terroir. We talked about the river level. If the Navarro River floods the bridge on Highway 128, no one goes home. The water was three feet below the road at dawn.

By three o'clock, wind picked up from the coast. Vines look like skeletons after pruning. They are bare and jagged. You see the architecture of the vineyard. Without leaves, you see the mistakes of previous years. A crooked trunk stays crooked. You fix what you can with a hand saw. The sound of metal on dry wood is sharp.

The sap runs when the ground warms. It drips from the cut ends. This is bleeding. It means the roots are awake. We finished forty rows. My forearms throbbed. I scraped clay off my soles with a flathead screwdriver before getting into the car. The valley looked grey and silent.

I drove past the Boonville General Store. People inside sat near the wood stove. They wore clean sweaters. I looked at my fingernails. They were stained black from tannins in the dormant wood. That stain stays for a week. It does not wash off with soap. You have to wear it.

I opened a bottle from the 2021 vintage at home. It came from this block. The wine tasted like pomegranate and dried needles. It did not taste like mud. It did not taste like wet wool or cramped hands. The glass hides the labor. People pay eighty dollars to ignore the screwdriver I used on my boots. They see the label but they do not see the moss on the trunks.
